What to expect at your first anti-wrinkle appointment

April Waterman · May 2026 · 4 min read

If you're considering anti-wrinkle injections for the first time, you're probably circling around the same questions everyone does. Will it hurt. Will it look obvious. What if I hate it. The answers are nearly always more reassuring than the imagined version, and the first appointment is more conversation than treatment. Here's what actually happens.

Before you arrive

The medical history form I send out before your appointment is longer than you might expect. I want to know about medications, allergies, recent dental work, anything that's gone on in the last six weeks I should factor in. Please fill it in honestly — there's nothing on it that will be judged, and a complete picture is what allows me to recommend something genuinely suited to your face rather than something off a menu.

If you're on a course of antibiotics, recovering from a cold sore, pregnant or breastfeeding, the appointment will be rebooked. None of these are deal-breakers; they're just signals that today isn't the day.

The consultation

For first-time clients, the appointment is half consultation, half treatment. We'll spend the first twenty minutes or so just talking — about what bothers you, what you've tried, and just as importantly, what you don't want. I'll often ask you to make a series of expressions so I can see how your face moves naturally. The aim isn't to remove movement. It's to soften the lines that movement creates over time, while leaving your expressions intact.

If I don't think anti-wrinkle treatment is the right fit for what you're describing, I'll tell you. I'd rather you book the appointment that suits you than the one you booked.

If a different approach would serve you better — skin boosters, different timing, or simply a watching brief — I'll say so during the consultation. There is no pressure to proceed on the day.

The treatment itself

The injections take roughly five to ten minutes. The needles are very fine — finer than the ones used to take blood — and most people describe the sensation as a brief pinch with each injection point. There are usually somewhere between three and seven points, depending on which areas we've agreed to treat.

You'll see a small raised bump at each site, like a mosquito bite, that settles within fifteen to thirty minutes. Some people get a tiny bruise; most don't. You can wear makeup the same evening. Most clients go straight back to work or pick the children up from school afterwards. There's nothing to recover from in the dramatic sense.

The bit nobody tells you

The result doesn't appear on the way out the door. Anti-wrinkle injections take two to three days to begin working and around two weeks to reach their full effect. The first week is the most disorienting because, if you've been watching closely, you might convince yourself nothing has changed.

This is when I sometimes get an anxious message on day three. Nothing has gone wrong; day three is too early. Wait until day fourteen, look in the mirror in normal daylight, then we'll talk. I always offer a complimentary review at the two-week mark — if anything needs nudging, that's when we do it.

What "natural" actually looks like

This is the part most clients are nervous about, so I'll be specific. After good treatment, you should still be able to raise your eyebrows. You should still be able to frown if you want to. Your forehead should not look shrink-wrapped. Friends should tell you that you look well-rested, not that you look "done."

If the only adjective people reach for is frozen or smooth, the treatment has been pushed too far, and that's an aesthetic choice as much as a clinical one. My choice is to treat conservatively, see you back at two weeks, and add more if we both agree it's needed. Subtraction is harder than addition.

Aftercare, briefly

For the first four hours: no lying flat, no exercise, no rubbing the area. For the first twenty-four hours: no facials, saunas, or alcohol. After that, life continues. You'll get a written copy of all of this on the way out.

If you've read this far and you're still on the fence, the best next step isn't booking treatment — it's booking a consultation. They're free, they take about twenty minutes, and you leave with a plan rather than a syringe. Book one here.
